A Practical AI Governance Framework for Small Businesses

AI governance isn’t only for large enterprises. Here are four practical steps small businesses can take today to manage AI risk without adding bureaucracy.

Jack Kimrey · July 14, 2026 · 4 min read

Why Every Business Using AI Is Already Managing Risk

Every time someone on your team uses ChatGPT to draft an email, uses an AI tool to summarize a document, or automates a workflow with a no-code AI platform — a governance decision is being made. The question isn’t whether your business has AI governance. It’s whether that governance is intentional or accidental.

Most small businesses fall into the second category. That’s not a criticism — it’s the natural result of tools advancing faster than policies. But “we’ll deal with it later” is getting harder to justify as AI becomes more embedded in daily operations.

The Misconception Holding Small Businesses Back

When most people hear “AI governance,” they picture a Fortune 500 legal department issuing a 40-page policy document. That mental model keeps small businesses from starting at all.

The reality is different. Governance at a small business level doesn’t mean bureaucracy — it means having clear, shared answers to a handful of basic questions. Who decides which AI tools we use? What data are we allowed to put into them? When do we need a human to review an AI output before acting on it? How do we document what we’re doing in case something goes wrong?

Those four questions are the foundation. Everything else builds from there.

Four Essentials to Put in Place Now

1. Approved Tools List

Create a short list of AI tools your team is permitted to use for work purposes. It doesn’t need to be exhaustive — just clear. The goal is to prevent employees from unknowingly using tools that don’t meet your data handling requirements. For most small businesses, this list is 3–5 tools.

Practical starting point

Ask every team member to tell you which AI tools they’re currently using for work. You may be surprised. That list becomes the basis for your approved tools review.

2. Data Handling Rules

Specify what categories of information should never be entered into AI tools. At a minimum: customer personally identifiable information (PII), protected health information (PHI) if relevant, confidential client data, and any proprietary business information. This rule doesn’t need to be sophisticated. “Don’t paste customer data into ChatGPT” is a perfectly good policy for most small businesses to start with.

3. Human Review Requirements

Identify the categories of decisions or outputs where a human must review AI work before it’s acted on. Draft communications going to clients? Review required. Internal brainstorming notes? Probably fine. Medical or legal information? Non-negotiable human review. Financial projections? Review required. The category list should reflect your actual risk exposure.

4. Basic Documentation

Maintain a lightweight record of which AI tools you use, what they’re used for, and what data they touch. This isn’t about legal protection — it’s about operational clarity. When a problem arises (and eventually one will), having a record makes it much easier to understand what happened and fix it.

The NIST AI RMF: A Framework Worth Understanding

The National Institute of Standards and Technology published its AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) to help organizations govern AI use across four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage.

For small businesses, the most immediately useful element is the Govern function — establishing the policies, accountabilities, and culture that make AI risk management stick. The NIST framework is free and publicly available, and it’s built for organizations of all sizes.

You don’t need to implement the full framework on day one. But borrowing its vocabulary and basic structure gives you a defensible foundation to build on.

What the SBA and Federal Policy Are Saying

The U.S. Small Business Administration has increasingly positioned AI adoption as a competitive issue for small businesses — and AI governance as part of responsible adoption. Their resources for small business owners on technology adoption are worth bookmarking.

At the federal level, the White House’s “Winning the Race: America’s AI Action Plan” (July 2025) reinforces a national posture that encourages AI adoption while emphasizing the importance of responsible use practices — particularly for businesses in regulated sectors.

None of this creates legal obligations directly for most small businesses today. But it signals the direction of travel, and being ahead of that curve is better than being behind it.

Start With One Page

The most actionable version of everything above fits on a single page. Call it your “AI Use Policy.” It should answer:

  • Which tools are approved for work use
  • What data may not be entered into AI tools
  • When human review is required before acting on AI output
  • Who is responsible for keeping this policy current

Share it with your team. Revisit it every six months. That’s a governance program. It doesn’t need to be more than that to be meaningful.

Why This Is Worth Doing Now

The businesses that establish basic AI governance now will be better positioned as requirements evolve — whether that’s regulatory, contractual (clients increasingly ask about AI policies), or simply operational. The cost of doing this thoughtfully today is low. The cost of cleaning up a preventable incident later is much higher.

Governance isn’t about slowing down AI adoption. It’s about adopting AI in a way you can defend, sustain, and build on.
